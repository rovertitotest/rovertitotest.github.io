Mechanical Configuration

Our first moon rover

The rover is compact and its surface is comparable to that of an A4 sheet. Inside you will find the battery, the electronics boards, the motors and the cameras necessary for the mission.
It features 6 sets of flight heritage engines plus planetary gearbox that drive wheels specially designed to navigate unfamiliar terrain.

Power

Survive one full lunar day!

Thanks to the advancement of materials engineering, today we choose to have highly efficient solar panel modules with negligible weight, which guarantee us all the necessary energy to move our mechatronic system. Another decisive factor in our design is the batteries, a fundamental element that determines the success of our mission. We opted for centralized energy storage. It provides us with regulated voltage outputs for our control and power requirements, as well as enough energy density to keep our CPU in a controlled atmosphere while it performs data logging during 336 hours of operation.

Navigation

How does our rover manage to move and locate itself at the same time without teleoperation?

Among the wide range of autonomous robot navigation algorithms, we are using a strategy based on Visual SLAM. Our system is composed of a main stereo camera in this way, we can detect relevant "features" of the environment and perform a highly accurate localization and mapping of the lunar landscape.
The correct use of SLAM together with planning algorithms will allow our Rover to make autonomous decisions and decide where it has to move, always making an inference of parameters such as obstacles, valleys and craters.

Communication

Wifi in space!

The rover has three communication systems. The first is RS-422. It is used to check the "health status" of the rover before the mission begins. The second is IEEE 802.11n (WiFi 4) which is used to communicate with the lander in zone A (200 m) and download all the collected information (geological and images). The third is LoRa, which lets the rovers communicate with each other within a 25 km radius (zone B) and transfer the collected information until the one closest to the lander can download that information. That way, the analysis area can be expanded.

Electronics

How is our rover controlled?

The brain of rover is a microcontroller of the family SAMV71, it will control different subsystems like power module, temperature control, communications and data processing. To bring out the processing required for Visual SLAM, we count with a SBC (Single Board Computer), where the SALM navigation libraries will be running. To achieve an easy implementation of the rover we pretend to use COTS (Commercial Of The Shelf) products, and open source libraries with the objective of reducing development and evaluation times and costs. Avoiding the use of rad-tolerant and rad-hardened components whenever possible, except for those developing a critical mission like the memories and the microcontroller. To achieve a good reliability the rover will have redundant components that start working when a critical component fails. We attempt to make a versatile product, adaptable to different missions with the least effort, and large scale manufacturable.
